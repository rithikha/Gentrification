Gentrification in the City of Vancouver

Discussion

This analysis had two main objectives, to see if our model could identify patterns in negative change over time that would indicate gentrification and to identify current areas in the City of Vancouver that are experiencing gentrification/are gentrified.

PATTERNS IN SPATIAL CHANGE

Table 1 below indicates a pattern in the development of negative change from T1 (2006-2011) to T2 (2011-2016) with 11 neighbourhoods categorized as either Gentrifying or Gentrified. All neighbourhoods classified as gentrifying in T1, transitioned into Gentrified in T2, with the exception of Downtown and the West End. These include:

  • Grandview Woodland
  • Hasting Sunrise
  • Fairview
  • Kensington Cedar Cottage
  • Killarney

Neighbourhoods classified as already Gentrified in T1, did not show any unusual amounts of negative change in T2, possibly indicating that a new baseline for population demographics has been set due to gentrification. These include:

  • Kitsilano
  • Oakridge
  • Arbutus Ridge

Strathcona and Hastings Sunrise remained classified as Gentrified and Gentrifying, respectively, although both the affected areas did expand in size implying that their is an increase in gentrification from T1 to T2. One anomaly was the West End which is classified as a mix of gentrified and gentrifying in T1 and then as gentrifying in T2. Various factors could account for this observation which are explained in further detail on the improvements page.

The Tt map visualizes negative change over a longer period of time (2006-2016). The results classify Strathcona, Oakridge, Kitsilano, Kensington Cedar Cottage, and Kerrisdale, as gentrifying, and Downtown, Hastings Sunrise, Fairview, Riley Park, and South Cambie as Gentrified, with South Cambie, and Kerrisdale being new additions. A few neighbourhoods which were identified as undergoing high amounts of negative change in T1 and T2, were classified as showing no concerning change in Tt. These include:

  • Arbutus Ridge
  • West End
  • Killarney
  • Renfrew Collingwood
  • Victoria FraserView
  • Sunset

These differences in classification indicate that the temporal units that negative change was measured with does affect the results of the analysis. While T1 shows most gentrifying and gentrified areas as occurring in the Western/SouthWestern and Northern Sections of the city, T2 shows an eastern movement of these areas, and as expected Tt shows a combination of the two spatial patterns, with most of the gentrifying and gentrified areas occurring in the middle of the city,stretching diagonally from west to east.

LITERATURE CONFIRMATION

“Displacement is and always will be vital to an understanding of gentrification, in terms of retaining definitional coherence and of retaining a critical perspective on the process”
- Tom Slater

Other literature was used to confirm that the neighbourhoods identified as gentrifying or gentified in our study have in reality been classified similarly by other sources. While there are many studies on Gentrification in Vancouver, the majority focus on the Downtown Eastside. Our confirmation sources not only include academic research, but also journalistic pieces, interviews, reports produced by various levels of city and neighbourhood government, as well as public opinion/observations found online such as Yelp Reviews. 13, 14

While observations that do not rely heavily on hard data (like most academic research) may seem unreliable, the communities being displaced are also the ones that should be given the strongest say in what constitutes as a gentrified area. Unfortunately they are also the populations who are most often misrepresented through hard data alone as Kathe Newman and Elvin Wyly 15 explain:

“In short it is difficult to find people who have been displaced, particularly if those people are poor… By definition, displaced residents have disappeared from the very places where researchers and census-takers go to look for them.”

While data and formal academic research can be incredibly insightful, for multifaceted issues such as gentrification, these insights need to be tempered with more qualitative, personal forms of observation and analysis. “In other words,” as BMW Guggenheim Fellow and Journalist Christina Mclaren writes, “the story of gentrification [ ] will not be told in numbers.” 16

Out of the 18 neighbourhoods identified as gentrifying or gentrified in all three time periods (T1, T2, and Tt), our literature review confirmed 15 neighborhoods as experiencing gentrification or some kind of gentrifying change. Victoria Fraserview and Sunset did not have sufficient gentrification information other than surge in property value (which could be an indicator of oncoming gentrification). The second neighbourhood with insufficient confirmation was Kerrisdale. While it is known to have been gentrified in the 1980’s, no further evidence of current day gentrification was found. Tt.

The literature reviews also confirmed that the smaller temporal units provided more representative results of gentrification occurring in the city as 6 neighbourhoods were classified as experiencing normal change in Tt when literature confirmed that these areas have/are in fact experiencing significant negative changes.
